The Textile

Chanderi Silk —
Light Made Fabric

Chanderi fabric originates from the ancient town of Chanderi in the Ashoknagar district of Madhya Pradesh — a place where Bundela kings once commissioned cloths so fine that legend held they could be folded and passed through a finger ring.

Suvarna Chanderi is a contemporary product concept that reimagines this heirloom textile as a curated home and lifestyle collection: stoles, table runners, wall hangings, and cushion covers — each piece a singular work of woven art bearing a GI-tagged certificate of authentic origin.

The fabric is a luminous blend of pure silk warp and cotton weft, giving it its signature translucency — gossamer-light, yet warm to the touch, draping like whispered silk against the skin.

The Palette

Colors of the
Betwa Valley

Traditional Chanderi draws its palette from the landscape that surrounds it — the saffron of temple flowers, the teal of monsoon ponds, the ivory of kansa vessels, and the deep rust of Bundelkhand laterite soil.

In the Suvarna collection, these are rendered in natural, azo-free dyes and real zari — a flat gold thread spun from fine silver coated with gold — creating a palette that deepens gracefully with age.

Cultural Significance

Three Centuries of
Woven Memory

Chanderi weaving finds its earliest mention in the 11th century, when it clothed the royalty of the Malwa Sultanate. The great Mughal emperor Akbar later patronised Chanderi looms, incorporating its silks into the imperial treasury of fine cloth.

In Bundela tradition, a Chanderi saree gifted to a bride was not merely a garment — it was a blessing woven in gold: an invocation of abundance, light, and the luminous possibility of a new life beginning.

Today, Chanderi holds a Geographical Indication (GI) tag from the Government of India — one of only a handful of textiles to receive this recognition — affirming its irreplaceable status as a living cultural inheritance, not merely a commodity.

The Suvarna collection channels this historical depth into contemporary forms, ensuring every purchase directly sustains a weaver family whose craft lineage may stretch back ten generations.

Artisan Craftsmanship

From Loom to
Living Room

01
Thread Preparation
Raw silk yarn is sourced from Karnataka and degummed by hand in Chanderi. Cotton weft threads are hand-spun on a charkha. Pure zari — silver wire coated with gold — is sourced from Surat's hereditary zari makers. Each thread is starched, stretched, and wound onto bobbins by hand before the loom is even touched.
02
Loom Setting & Jacquard Card Punching
Chanderi weavers use pit looms — looms sunk into the earth — that allow foot treadles to control up to 2,400 warp threads simultaneously. For patterned designs, a Jacquard attachment controlled by hand-punched cards lifts individual threads to form the motif. Programming one complex design can take a master weaver two full days.
03
Hand Weaving & Finishing
A single stole of 2.5 metres may take three to five days of continuous weaving. Zari borders are woven simultaneously, not added later, requiring perfect rhythm between both hands. Finishing includes hand-washing in cold water, drying in filtered shade, and a final quality check by the master weaver who signs the piece with a handwoven weaver's mark in the selvedge.
Sustainability

A Textile That
Leaves No Shadow

Chanderi weaving is among the most low-impact luxury textile processes on earth. The pit loom requires no electricity. Natural dyes — indigo, pomegranate rind, turmeric, madder root — biodegrade fully. Waste thread is composted or repurposed into buti embroidery by women artisans in the same cluster.

The Suvarna collection is packaged in hand-stitched unbleached cotton pouches and recycled mulberry paper — zero plastic, traceable from loom to doorstep.
